Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by *Plasmodium parasites* transmitted through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. It remains a major health concern in tropical and subtropical regions, affecting millions every year. Symptoms include high fever, chills, sweating, headache, and fatigue. Severe malaria can cause anemia, organ failure, or even death if not treated early.

Preventing malaria involves protecting yourself from mosquito bites and reducing mosquito breeding areas:
  • Sleep under insecticide-treated mosquito nets.
  • Use mosquito repellents on skin and clothing.
  • Wear long-sleeved clothing, especially in the evening.
  • Eliminate stagnant water sources around your home.
  • Take prescribed preventive medicines if traveling to high-risk areas.

Malaria is caused by *Plasmodium parasites* (commonly P. falciparum and P. vivax). Transmission occurs when an infected female Anopheles mosquito bites a person, injecting parasites into their bloodstream. Rarely, malaria can also spread through blood transfusion, organ transplant, or from mother to child during pregnancy.

Early diagnosis is crucial for effective treatment. Malaria is typically diagnosed by examining blood smears under a microscope or using rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs).

Treatments depend on the type of Plasmodium parasite:
  • Artemisinin-based Combination Therapies (ACTs) are most effective for P. falciparum.
  • Chloroquine or primaquine may be used for P. vivax or P. ovale.
  • Severe malaria cases may require hospitalization and intravenous medication.
